Life Calling Discovery
What is in your tool belt?
God has endowed every person with a unique combination of gifts, talents, experiences, and passions for his purposes. This worksheet helps you examine the tools he has placed in your belt — not to manufacture a calling, but to discover the one he has already been shaping in you.
Personal reflection
60 – 90 min
Take your time. Be honest.

The foundation of this exercise
"The spheres are not a motivation. Your calling is not a target. They are a description of how you express your relationship with God."
Life Calling Workshop — YWAM

This is a discovery exercise, not a test. There are no wrong answers and no expected outcomes. The goal is not to arrive at a five-year plan — it is to look honestly at how God has been shaping you, and to begin to see the patterns. The person you are reading these words is already a person God has been forming for a specific purpose in a specific corner of the world. This worksheet creates space to see what he has been doing.

The Ten Categories
Work through each one slowly.

Don't rush to get to the synthesis at the end. The most important discoveries tend to come in the categories you least expect. Give yourself permission to write honestly — not the answer that sounds most spiritual, but what is actually true.

1
Motivation
What enflames your heart?
Not what you think should motivate you. What actually does.
Motivation is one of the most reliable indicators of calling — because it is hard to manufacture and hard to sustain artificially. What makes you come alive? What would you do even if nobody paid you for it, and even if nobody was watching? Pay attention to what stirs something in you that feels like more than preference — the thing that genuinely burdens you, energises you, or keeps coming back.
Reflect and respond
What topics, problems, or people make you lose track of time when you are thinking about them or working on them?
What injustice or brokenness in the world makes you genuinely angry — the thing you cannot just walk past?
When do you feel most alive? What were you doing the last time you thought "this is what I was made for"?
2
Life History
What has God been doing in your story?
Your history is not a list of events. It is a shaped narrative.
God wastes nothing. The turning points in your life — the losses, the unexpected doors, the moments of clarity, the times when everything fell apart and something new emerged — are not random. They are the material God has been using to shape the specific person your calling requires you to be. Joseph did not understand the pit until he was in the palace. You may not yet see the full picture of your story. But looking at the major chapters is one of the most powerful ways to see what God has been building.
Reflect and respond
What are the three or four major turning points in your life — the moments that changed the direction of who you were becoming?
What experiences — painful, joyful, or surprising — have most shaped how you see the world and what you care about?
Looking back, where do you see God's hand in your story — even in the places that felt like detours at the time?
3
Natural Talents
What are you genuinely good at?
The things that come easily to you — that others find difficult.
Natural talents are often undervalued precisely because they come easily — we tend to discount what does not cost us effort. But God did not distribute gifts randomly. The things you do well without trying are often the core tools of your calling. Romans 12:6 says: "We have different gifts, according to the grace given to each of us." The Greek word is charisma — grace-gifts. What others would need years of training to do, you carry naturally. That is not accident — it is equipment.
Reflect and respond
What do people consistently come to you for — the things others ask your help with before they ask anyone else?
What comes naturally to you that you notice others find difficult? What do you do almost without thinking that others have to work hard to learn?
What have you been told — by teachers, parents, coaches, or peers — that you are particularly gifted at?
4
Personality
How has God wired you?
Your personality is not a limitation to work around. It is part of the design.
God made introverts and extroverts, big-picture thinkers and detail-oriented implementers, people who thrive in crisis and people who build slowly over decades. None of these is more spiritual than the others. Your personality shapes which expression of the Kingdom is most natural for you — and often points toward the kind of environment and role where you will be most fruitful. Psalm 139:14 says you are "fearfully and wonderfully made." Your wiring is not accidental.
Reflect and respond
Do you tend to draw energy from being with people, or from being alone? How does this shape where you thrive?
Are you more energised by big-picture vision, or by the practical details of making things work?
What are two or three personality strengths that have served you well in your most effective moments?
What environments — types of work, settings, team dynamics — bring out the best version of you?
5
Spiritual Gifts
What is God blessing and multiplying through you?
Not what you do for God — what he seems to be doing through you.
Spiritual gifts are not performances you put on for God. They are the Spirit's activity through you — and the clearest indicator is usually fruit. Where do you see God moving when you engage? Where do people encounter God through your presence, your words, your acts? 1 Peter 4:10 says: "Each of you should use whatever gift you have received to serve others, as faithful stewards of God's grace in its various forms." The key word is received — these gifts are grace, not achievement.
Reflect and respond
Where do you consistently see God move when you are involved — the contexts or activities where things seem to come alive in a way that goes beyond your effort?
What do others say they receive when they are with you — the impact they describe that you may not always see in yourself?
If you have a sense of your spiritual gifts — whether through formal assessment or life experience — what are they? Where have you seen them confirmed?
6
Enculturation
What has your culture given you — and what has it cost you?
Your cultural background is not neutral. It is both a gift and a lens.
Every culture reflects some aspects of God's character and distorts others. Your cultural formation has given you genuine strengths — a sense of hospitality, an awareness of community, a particular way of seeing justice or beauty or family — that other cultures lack. It has also given you blindspots and patterns that need to be examined. Paul writes in Romans 12:2: "Do not conform to the pattern of this world" — this is as much about cultural formation as it is about individual sin. Knowing both sides of your enculturation helps you steward it well.
Reflect and respond
What are the genuine strengths of your cultural background — the values, perspectives, or capacities it has given you?
What are the blindspots or limitations your cultural formation has given you — the patterns that may need examination?
How might your specific cultural background be uniquely suited to bring the Kingdom into a particular context or community?
7
Training
What have you learned — formally and informally?
Education, experience, and the things life has taught you by doing them.
Training includes the obvious — formal education, courses, qualifications — but also the less obvious: the skills you developed through years of doing something, the knowledge you absorbed in a particular industry, the wisdom you earned through failure. Nehemiah assessed the walls of Jerusalem before he rebuilt them (Nehemiah 2:11–15). He did a quiet inventory of what was there. This section is your Nehemiah moment — an honest survey of what you have and what you still need.
Reflect and respond
What formal training or education do you have? What did it equip you with — beyond the qualification itself?
What skills have you developed through life experience — the things you learned by doing, failing, and trying again?
What training or skills do you sense you still need — the gaps between where you are and where you sense your calling is taking you?
8
Interests
What locations, people, and needs pull at you?
Where does your heart go when you let it wander?
Interests are not trivial. They are often God's way of directing our attention before we have the language for calling. A persistent fascination with a particular people group, a region of the world, a human need, or a type of work is worth taking seriously. Proverbs 4:23 says "guard your heart, for everything you do flows from it." What your heart is drawn toward — without being told — is significant data about what it was made for.
Reflect and respond
Are there specific locations — cities, nations, regions, neighbourhoods — that you find yourself drawn to, praying for, or wanting to understand better?
Are there specific people groups — defined by age, culture, need, profession, or marginalisation — that you consistently feel drawn toward?
What human needs — poverty, education, mental health, environmental care, political corruption, family breakdown — keep surfacing in your reading, your conversations, your prayers?
9
Stations
What are the fixed things that shape your context?
The realities that are not preferences — they are the current framework of your life.
Stations include the things about your life that are not really choices — your gender, your marital status, your family responsibilities, your nationality, your life stage. These are not limitations on your calling. They are part of its shape. Martin Luther's famous insight was that the Christian life is lived in the stations God has placed us in — the ordinary roles and relationships through which his Kingdom comes. Understanding your stations honestly helps you understand where your calling will actually be expressed, not where it might be in some other life.
Reflect and respond
What is your current relational station — single, married, parent, caregiver? How does this shape where and how you can serve?
What other fixed factors shape your current context — nationality, health, family obligations, financial situation?
How do your current stations open doors for Kingdom impact that someone in a different station could not access?
10
Connections
Who is in your network — and who covers your gaps?
No calling is meant to be lived alone. The body of Christ is a team.
Calling is rarely solitary. Nehemiah did not rebuild Jerusalem alone — he had gatekeepers and goldsmiths and merchants and priests, each working on the section nearest their home (Nehemiah 3). Paul had Barnabas and Silas and Timothy and Priscilla and Aquila — each bringing what the others lacked. Your network is not just professional capital. It is part of how God intends the calling to be carried. And your gaps — the areas where you are weakest — are often invitations to find the people whose strengths cover them.
Reflect and respond
Who are the key people God has placed in your life — mentors, peers, team members — who have shaped or supported your calling?
What are your areas of genuine weakness — the capacities you lack that your calling requires? Who in your network covers these gaps?
Are there others who share your specific burden? Who else is carrying a similar passion or calling — and how could your paths connect?
The Pattern
Look at what has emerged.

Now look across everything you have written. Calling is rarely revealed in a single answer — it emerges in the pattern. What threads run through multiple categories? What surprises you? What has God been consistently pointing to that you may have dismissed?

Your Life Calling — First Draft
This will change. That is fine. Write what you can see right now.
What surprised you in this exercise? What emerged that you were not expecting?
What are the two or three strongest threads running across multiple categories?
What do you sense God saying to you through this reflection?
How would you describe your calling in one or two sentences — however tentative?
What are one or two concrete next steps — education, training, conversation, or action — that would move you further into what you have sensed?
Calling is not a destination you arrive at — it is a direction you walk in. The tools in your belt are already there. God has been placing them since before you were born (Jeremiah 1:5). The question this worksheet is really asking is: will you look honestly at what he has already given you, and trust that it is enough for what he is asking you to do?

Use this alongside the Lord's Prayer Spheres worksheet — bring your calling into his presence, and let the prayer shape how you understand where your tools belong.
